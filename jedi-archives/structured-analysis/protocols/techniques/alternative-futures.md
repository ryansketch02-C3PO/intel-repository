# Protocol: Alternative Futures Analysis

> **Phase**: Foresight | **Effort**: 2 – 4 hours | **Bias Mitigated**: Linear thinking, availability bias, single-outcome fixation
> **Output Artifact**: `working/alt-futures.md`
> **Library**: 01-tradecraft-primer §3, 05-taxonomy #48

---

## Purpose

Construct a 2x2 matrix of four plausible futures based on two critical uncertain forces. This technique combats the tendency to fixate on a single "most likely" outcome by forcing exploration of multiple plausible trajectories and developing indicators for each.

---

## Execution

### 1. SETUP
- Read ALL prior technique outputs in the `working/` directory
- Identify the focal issue: what decision or question is this analysis trying to inform?
- Define the time horizon (e.g., 6 months, 1 year, 5 years)
- Gather relevant evidence on driving forces and uncertainties

### 2. PRIME
State: "I will now explore four distinct plausible futures. The goal is not to predict which future is most likely, but to prepare for multiple possibilities and identify early indicators of which trajectory is unfolding."

### 3. EXECUTE

1. **Define the focal issue and time horizon** — state clearly:
   - What is the central question? (from analysis context)
   - What time horizon are we exploring?
   - What is the scope? (geographic, sectoral, organizational)
2. **Brainstorm critical forces** — identify 6-10 forces that will shape the future:
   - For each force: name, description, current trajectory, uncertainty level (High/Medium/Low)
   - Forces should span categories: political, economic, technological, social, military, environmental
   - Draw from prior technique outputs (KAC assumptions, Cross-Impact variables, etc.)
3. **Select the two most uncertain and impactful forces** — these become the matrix axes:
   - Both must be genuinely uncertain (not trending clearly in one direction)
   - Both must be high-impact (significantly change outcomes regardless of other forces)
   - Each axis has two poles (e.g., "Cooperation ↔ Confrontation", "Rapid change ↔ Gradual change")
   - Justify why these two were selected over alternatives
4. **Construct the 2x2 matrix** — four quadrants, each a distinct future:
   - Name each future vividly (memorable title, not "Quadrant 1")
   - Write a narrative for each (1-2 paragraphs): what happened, how it unfolded, what the world looks like at the time horizon
   - Futures must be plausible — supported by evidence and internal logic
   - Futures must be distinct — each tells a genuinely different story
5. **Develop signposts and indicators** — for each future:
   - 3-5 observable indicators that would signal this future is materializing
   - Indicators must be specific and monitorable (not vague trends)
   - Distinguish leading indicators (early signal) from lagging indicators (confirmation)
6. **Strategic implications** — for each future:
   - What decisions or preparations does this future warrant?
   - What is robust across multiple futures? (hedging strategies)
   - What is specific to one future? (contingency plans)

### 4. ARTIFACT
Write `working/alt-futures.md` using the Alternative Futures template.

### 5. FINDINGS
Summarize: the two selected axes, four futures by name, key cross-cutting implications, and the most critical indicators to monitor.

### 6. HANDOFF
Pass signposts to Monitoring Plan generation. Pass strategic implications to report generator.

---

## Watch-Outs
- **Unitarity check**: If assigning probability estimates to futures, they must sum to ~1.0 (MECE). If they don't, either futures overlap (not mutually exclusive) or a plausible future is missing (not collectively exhaustive). Adjust the matrix before proceeding.
- **Designating a "most likely" future**: Resist the urge. The purpose is to explore the space, not collapse it. If pressed, you may note which future current evidence favors, but frame it as "current trajectory" not "prediction."
- **Aspirational or worst-case bias**: Futures should be plausible, not wishful or catastrophic. Avoid making one quadrant the "good" future and another the "bad" future — each should have mixed implications.
- **Axis selection is critical**: Poor axis selection produces redundant or implausible futures. Both axes must be genuinely uncertain AND high-impact. If one axis is trending clearly, it's a driver, not an uncertainty.
- **Narrative vividness matters**: Abstract futures ("increased tensions") are less useful than vivid ones ("a maritime incident in the South China Sea triggers..."). Specificity enables better indicator development.
- **Integration with prior techniques**: Use Cross-Impact Matrix variables as force candidates. Use KAC assumptions to identify uncertainties. Use What-If scenarios as potential future narratives.
